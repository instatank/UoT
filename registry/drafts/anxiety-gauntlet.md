## 1. Rehearsal billed as the event

**The moving part (one breath):** an uncertain future event gets flagged as
threat → the mind simulates it → the body responds to each simulation at
nearly the event's full price → the relief of "having prepared" reinforces
another run.

**Recognition lines:**
- "I rehearse conversations that haven't happened yet."
- "It's Tuesday, and Friday has already happened forty times."
- "I arrive at everything already exhausted by the version of it I invented."

**Attestations:**
- Stoicism — Seneca, *Letters to Lucilius* 13 ("we suffer more often in
  imagination than in reality" — verify exact wording against a Tier 1
  translation; the letter's argument is squarely this mechanism).
- Buddhism — *Madhupiṇḍika Sutta*, MN 18 (papañca: one feeling proliferated
  into futures that then "beset" their maker).
- Christianity — Matthew 6:34 ("sufficient unto the day"; tomorrow's evil
  billed today).
- Neuroscience/Psychology — anticipatory anxiety recruiting the same threat
  circuitry as the event (Grupe & Nitschke 2013, *Nat. Rev. Neurosci.*);
  predictive-processing accounts (Clark; Barrett).

**Notes:** currently the spine of the `anxiety-first-date` seed session, where
it is *fused* with candidate #8 under the ad-hoc name "anticipatory
self-surveillance" — if both survive, the seed name retires and splits. Known
Near Miss on file: *premeditatio malorum* (deliberate, brief, from a settled
state, aimed at retiring attachment — opposite direction of fit).
**Overlap flag:** #7 (both future-leaning; distinct joints — #1 is simulation
under threat, #7 is attention posture without threat).

## 2. Vigilance mistaken for control

**The moving part:** scanning for danger *feels like* doing something about
it → most feared outcomes never arrive → the scanning takes the credit → the
loop is paid in relief and entrenched; stopping now feels like negligence.

**Recognition lines:**
- "If I stop worrying, it feels like taking my hands off the wheel."
- "My worry is the only thing holding the plane up."
- "Relaxing feels irresponsible."

**Attestations:**
- Stoicism — Epictetus, *Enchiridion* 1–2 (the dichotomy: outcomes are not in
  our power; the vigilance administers territory it doesn't govern).
- Christianity — Matthew 6:27 (the cubit: worry's measurable yield is zero);
  Psalm 127:2 ("he gives sleep to his beloved" — verify translation).
- Taoism — *Daodejing* 48 (wu-wei: "less and less is done, until nothing is
  left undone" — verify chapter attribution in a Tier 1 edition).
- Neuroscience/Psychology — Borkovec's avoidance/negative-reinforcement theory
  of worry; checking-behavior reinforcement literature.

**Tension candidate (record if admitted):** the traditions genuinely disagree
on the *replacement* for vigilance — Stoic prosochē is disciplined attention
(an effort), Taoist wu-wei is non-doing (effort's absence), 1 Peter 5:7 is
surrender to a person ("cast all your anxiety on him"). Same diagnosis, three
incompatible prescriptions. This is exactly what the Tensions ledger is for.

## 3. The alarm rings before the story

**The moving part:** arousal arrives first — heart, gut, breath — with no
narrative attached → the mind searches for a reason → any plausible threat is
hired → the story then cites the arousal as its evidence, and there are two
problems where there was one.

**Recognition lines:**
- "I wake up anxious and go looking for the reason."
- "My body panics first; my mind files the paperwork after."
- "Any worry will do — the dread was already there before the topic."

**Attestations:**
- Stoicism — Seneca, *De Ira* II.3–4 (first movements are involuntary; assent
  builds the passion) — stated for anger, explicitly generalized by the
  tradition to fear; Aulus Gellius, *Attic Nights* 19.1 (the Stoic philosopher
  pale in the storm, explaining propatheiai).
- Buddhism — *Sallatha Sutta*, SN 36.6 (the second arrow; vedanā precedes the
  craving/aversion that elaborates it).
- Sufism — the khawāṭir doctrine (classification of passing thoughts before
  the heart assents; al-Ghazālī / al-Qushayrī — **pointer unverified**, needs a
  specific locus during Registry build).
- Neuroscience/Psychology — interoceptive inference: affect precedes and
  shapes appraisal (Barrett, constructed emotion; Seth, interoceptive
  predictive coding).

**Notes:** shares the first-movements passage family with the anger seed
mechanism (`mech.assent-replay-amplification`) — same joint, different limb.
**Cross-category flag:** plausibly also Anger and Reactivity; fine at 2
categories, watch it.

## 4. Certainty bought with catastrophe

**The moving part:** not-knowing is more aversive than known-bad → the mind
adopts the worst case as "the realistic forecast" because a settled disaster
feels safer than an open question → planning proceeds against the disaster →
the open future closes and the disaster is lived now, on credit.

**Recognition lines:**
- "I'd rather know it'll go wrong than not know how it'll go."
- "The worst case feels like the honest one."
- "Hope feels naive; dread feels responsible."

**Attestations:**
- Neuroscience/Psychology — intolerance of uncertainty as a core anxiety
  driver (Carleton; Dugas & Ladouceur).
- Taoism — the "maybe" farmer parable is the obvious pointer **and it is
  folk-attributed**: the closest classical source is *Huainanzi* 18 (the old
  man who lost his horse), not Zhuangzi. Use Huainanzi or drop the lineage.
- Christianity — Exodus 16 (manna: provision issued per day, spoils if
  hoarded against tomorrow); Matthew 6:34.
- Buddhism — clinging to fixed outcomes vs. anicca — **no sharp passage in
  hand**; candidate sourcing needed or the lineage doesn't count.

**Notes:** weakest attestation set of the eight — may fail rule 1 at
verification. Admit only if sourcing firms up; failing the gauntlet here would
be the process working. Near Miss adjacency: premeditatio again (deliberate
worst-case rehearsal from a settled state ≠ compulsive worst-case adoption
from an unsettled one).

## 5. The invisible jury

**The moving part:** an imagined audience is convened → its verdict is treated
as your worth, live → every act becomes evidence in the trial → stakes rise,
spontaneity dies, performance degrades → the degraded performance is entered
into evidence.

**Recognition lines:**
- "There's a jury in every room that only I can see."
- "I hear the review while I'm still talking."
- "I don't meet people; I audition for them."

**Attestations:**
- Stoicism — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* 12.4 (we love ourselves more than
  others, yet weigh their opinions more than our own).
- Buddhism — *Lokavipatti Sutta*, AN 8.6 (the eight worldly winds — praise and
  blame as weather the untrained mind is spun by).
- Christianity — Galatians 1:10 ("am I now seeking the approval of man?");
  Matthew 6:1 (righteousness performed to be seen).
- Sufism — riyāʾ (action corrupted by being-seen; al-Ghazālī, *Iḥyāʾ*, the
  book on ostentation — locus to verify).
- Neuroscience/Psychology — sociometer theory (Leary); the spotlight effect
  (Gilovich & Savitsky).

**Notes:** five lineages attest — the richest candidate. **Cross-category
flag:** also Shame and Self-Judgment, arguably its center of mass; decide
which category owns it and which borrows it (2 categories is fine).

## 6. Relief that proves the danger

**The moving part:** the feared thing is avoided → relief follows → relief is
read as evidence of a near-miss ("good thing I didn't") → the alarm is
credited and trained → the map of safe territory shrinks and calls itself
prudence.

**Recognition lines:**
- "Every time I cancel, the relief feels like proof I was right to."
- "I've never found out it would have been fine, because I've never let it happen."
- "My world keeps getting smaller and calling it safety."

**Attestations:**
- Neuroscience/Psychology — safety behaviors maintaining anxiety (Salkovskis);
  exposure / inhibitory learning (Craske) — the best-attested anxiety
  mechanism in the clinical literature.
- Stoicism — Seneca, *Letters* 13 and 24 (fears examined and faced deliberately
  rather than dodged; "some things torment us before they ought").
- Christianity — Matthew 25:24–25 (the buried talent: "I was afraid, and went
  and hid your talent in the ground" — fear's avoidance presented as prudence
  and judged as the actual loss). Stronger fit than the usual 1 John 4:18.
- Buddhism — aversion (paṭigha) as a binding response that entrenches suffering
  (SN 36.6 covers the move) — **serviceable but not sharp; better passage
  wanted** during Registry build.

**Notes:** empirically bulletproof, contemplatively thinner — rule 1 risk is
real. If it survives, it pairs naturally with exposure-shaped practices.

## 7. Living one minute ahead

**The moving part:** attention leans permanently into the next moment → the
present registers as a corridor to somewhere else → felt life thins → the
thinness itself reads as unease → the lean steepens.

**Recognition lines:**
- "I'm always slightly ahead of my own life."
- "Even good moments feel like waiting rooms."
- "I spend the whole walk arriving."

**Attestations:**
- Buddhism — *Bhaddekaratta Sutta*, MN 131 ("do not chase the past or long for
  the future… ardently doing what should be done today").
- Stoicism — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* 2.14 (the present is the only
  thing one can lose), 8.36 (don't compound the future into now).
- Christianity — Matthew 6:11 with 6:34 (bread and evil both issued daily).
- Neuroscience/Psychology — Killingsworth & Gilbert 2010 ("a wandering mind is
  an unhappy mind"); default-mode-network prospection literature.
- Taoism — presence pointers exist (DDJ 14; Zhuangzi's flow passages) but are
  **diffuse; pick one during verification or drop the lineage.**

**Notes:** **Overlap flag with #1** is the real question: is this a distinct
moving part (attention posture, no threat required — it runs during *good*
moments) or the theme underneath #1 (threat simulation)? The good-moments case
argues it's distinct. **Cross-category flag:** also Disconnection.

## 8. The watcher splits the actor

**The moving part:** stakes are felt → a self-monitor spins up to supervise
the performance → attention halves; the monitor consumes exactly the capacity
the performance needs → the performance dips → the dip confirms the stakes →
the monitor doubles down.

**Recognition lines:**
- "There are two of me in every room: one talking, one taking notes."
- "I watch myself from the third row."
- "I can do anything perfectly until someone's watching — including me."

**Attestations:**
- Stoicism — Epictetus, *Discourses* 2.13 (the lyre-player: skilled alone,
  trembling on stage — anxiety enters where desire attaches to the audience).
- Buddhism — MN 18 again, at a different joint: papañca requires a protagonist;
  the commentary can't run without a self to defend.
- Sufism — self-forgetting as the practice target: absorption in dhikr; fanāʾ
  as dissolution of the self-watcher (**pointer unverified** — needs a specific
  passage, likely al-Qushayrī's *Risāla*).
- Neuroscience/Psychology — self-focused attention in the Clark & Wells model
  of social anxiety; explicit-monitoring theory of choking (Beilock).

**Notes:** the other half of the seed session's fused mechanism (see #1).
Survival of both #1 and #8 = split the seed name; survival of one = the seed
session re-homes under the survivor.
